But the earth is moving in Sicily, and that is where we must start.

The situation in Niscemi, a town in the province of Caltanissetta, has graduated from a local emergency to a national political crisis. The technical numbers provided by *Il Sole Ventiquattro Ore* are terrifying: a landslide front of four and a half kilometers, involving 350 million cubic meters of earth. For context, the head of Civil Protection, Fabio Ciciliano, stated on SkyTG24 that this mass is nearly one and a half times the size of the landslide that caused the Vajont dam disaster in 1963. While thankfully no one has died, 1,300 people are displaced, the town is cracking apart, and the finger-pointing has begun in earnest.

The political fault lines are as deep as the geological ones. On the left, *La Repubblica* and the opposition parties are demanding immediate accountability, calling for the resignation of Civil Protection Minister Nello Musumeci—who was previously the Governor of Sicily—and demanding Premier Giorgia Meloni report to Parliament. Their narrative is one of criminal negligence and ignored warnings. Conversely, the government defense, led by *Libero* and *Il Giornale*, frames this as a legacy issue, a disaster thirty years in the making that the current administration has inherited, not caused. Governor Renato Schifani, attempting to dodge the blame, claimed in *Corriere della Sera* that the municipality never sent an alarm in the last nine years—a defense that sounds thin given the scale of the disaster.

But the real, structural conflict here isn't just about who ignored the cracks in the ground; it represents a brutal battle over resources. As the region cries out for emergency money to rebuild roads and stabilize hillsides, the Sicilian assembly voted to divert funds earmarked for Matteo Salvini’s crown jewel project—the Bridge over the Strait of Messina—to fix the hydrogeological instability instead. Salvini, the Minister of Infrastructure, immediately fired back, stating, "The Bridge funds are not to be touched." This sets up a vicious internal conflict within the Right: the ideological necessity of building a bridge to connect the island, versus the practical reality that the island itself is crumbling before the bridge can even be built.

Moving from the laws of physics to the laws of the land, the Constitutional Court has thrown a wrench into the government’s signature security agenda.

In a ruling that *La Stampa* and legal experts seek to highlight as a victory for due process, the Court declared that Article 187 of the Highway Code is partially illegitimate. Until yesterday, under the strict "drug driving" rules championed by Matteo Salvini, a driver could be convicted simply for testing positive for drug traces, even if those traces were from consumption days or weeks prior. The Court has ruled that this is unconstitutional: to be punished criminally, a driver must be in an actual "state of alteration" while behind the wheel.

This is a significant blow to the "law and order" narrative of the ruling coalition. While *Il Giornale* and *Libero* are downplaying the rebuke, focusing on the safety aspects, the opposition press equates this to a rejection of populist jurisprudence. It essentially forces the police to return to a more complex, evidential standard—proving impairment, not just presence—which changes the mechanics of law enforcement on Italian roads overnight.

Meanwhile, deep in the bureaucratic machinery of Rome, a quieter but equally significant institutional clash took place regarding the PNRR, the National Recovery and Resilience Plan.

According to *Il Fatto Quotidiano* and later confirmed by *Corriere della Sera*, the government intended to include a "shield" in the latest decree. This provision would have allowed entrepreneurs who had underpaid workers on PNRR projects to avoid paying arrears if they simply "fixed" the situation moving forward. It was a deregulation designed to speed up projects and protect businesses. However, the clause vanished before the decree reached the Cabinet. Sources indicate this was due to "moral suasion" from the Quirinale—the office of President Sergio Mattarella—which flagged the norm as unconstitutional and unjust to workers. It is a classic example of the "invisible hand" of the Italian presidency keeping the legislative machine within the guardrails, a story often missed if you only look at the shouting matches in Parliament.

But before we go, let's look up toward the heavens—or at least, the roof of St. Peter's Basilica.

In a move that blends the sacred with the distinctly profane, *ANSA* reports that plans are underway to expand a refreshment point on the roof of the Vatican, effectively creating a "Bistrot on the Dome." It seems that even in the Eternal City, the hospitality sector is the one religion that converts everyone.

Conciliazione vita-lavoro
Il tema della conciliazione tra vita professionale e vita privata è stato oggetto negli ultimi anni di particolare attenzione, sia da un punto di vista sociale che politico.Di seguito una panoramica sulle misure attualmente vigenti volte a facilitare la conciliazione dei tempi di vita con i tempi di lavoro, tra le quali assumono particolare rilievo quelle poste a tutela della maternità e della paternità (organicamente disciplinate dal D.Lgs. n. 151/2001) e per l'assistenza dei soggetti con disabilità (previste principalmente dalla legge n. 104/1992), nonché quelle che introducono misure economiche a sostegno della maternità.Altre disposizioni riguardano più strettamente il mondo del lavoro. Tra queste si segnalano, in particolare, quelle in tema di lavoro agile, disciplinato dalla Legge n. 81/2017 e di trasformazione del contratto di lavoro da tempo pieno a tempo parziale, ai sensi del D.Lgs. n. 81/2015.Il quadro normativo è stato di recente modificato dal D.Lgs. n. 105/2022 - di attuazione della direttiva (UE) 2019/1158 in tema di equilibrio tra attività professionale e vita familiare per i genitori e i prestatori di assistenza. Tali disposizioni, comprese quelle relative al congedo di paternità obbligatorio, per espressa previsione normativa si applicano anche ai dipendenti delle pubbliche amministrazioni, ferme restando le eventuali indicazioni operative fornite dal competente Dipartimento della funzione pubblica (cfr. Circ. INPS 122/2022).
