**SECTION 1: THE LEAD**

The front pages today are dominated by a clash over gender, identity, and the safety of women, splitting the country down a jagged ideological line.

The spark is a landmark ruling from a tribunal in La Spezia, which authorized a 13-year-old to legally change their gender identity. For the progressive press, particularly *La Repubblica* and *Fanpage*, this is a victory for civil rights and awareness, with *Fanpage* highlighting that the teenager had a "consolidated" identity despite their young age. However, the reaction from the Right is immediate and fierce. *La Verità* and *Il Giornale* frame this not as rights, but as a dangerous precipice, questioning the wisdom of allowing irreversible legal changes for a minor, with *Libero* amplifying fears of "gender ideology" taking root in the courts.

But this theoretical debate is happening against a backdrop of very physical violence. The news cycle is heavy with the report of yet another femicide in Cava de' Tirreni, near Salerno, where a man stabbed his partner to death before killing himself. While the mainstream agencies like *ANSA* report the grim facts, the political spin appears elsewhere. *Libero Quotidiano* and *Il Giornale* are aggressively highlighting a separate case in Cervia, where the Democratic Party mayor is under investigation for mistreating his wife. For the right-wing press, this is a distinct opportunity to attack the moral high ground of the Left, suggesting that the party which champions women's rights in public has a problem with them in private.

**SECTION 2: THE ROUNDUP**

Moving from the culture wars to the actual war, the economic cost of the conflict in Ukraine is becoming a wedge issue in the Italian press.

Following the stalemate in peace talks reported by *Corriere della Sera*—which notes that Moscow has frozen any idea of a trilateral summit—the right-wing press is looking at the receipt. *La Verità* leads with a provocative headline claiming that Ukrainian President Zelensky "costs us 220 euros a head," a narrative designed to stoke resentment among taxpayers. This comes as *Il Sole 24 Ore* reports that the government, desperate to balance the books, is slashing the "Development and Cohesion Fund," cutting resources for infrastructure in regions like Emilia-Romagna and Basilicata. The narrative is clear: the money for foreign wars is now visibly eating into domestic concrete.

Meanwhile, following up on yesterday's lead regarding the riots in Turin, the street battles have ended, but the blame game has just begun. The focus has shifted from the masked protesters to their high-profile defenders. *Libero* and *Il Giornale* are furious that popular cultural figures, including the cartoonist Zerocalcare, have expressed solidarity with the Askatasuna social center. The Right is framing this as the "intellectual elite" pampering violent extremists who injure police officers. *La Stampa*, the voice of the Turin establishment, takes a more somber tone, cleaning up the physical mess of the riots while noting that the political rift between the mayor and the central government is only widening.

But before we go, here is a quick scan of the rest of the newsstand:

First, the "Epstein Files" continue to trickle out, and the Italian press is fascinated by the American censorship. *Il Fatto Quotidiano* and *Adnkronos* both highlight that while new pages were released, key photographs—including one allegedly showing Donald Trump—have mysteriously vanished from the official docket, fueling fresh conspiracy theories.

Also, a moment of national relief on the ski slopes. After a season of injuries and doubts, Italian skiing superstar Sofia Goggia won the Super-G in Val d’Isère today. *Corriere della Sera* reports she was in tears at the finish line, calling it a "liberation."

And finally, winter has officially arrived, literally. *Il Post* marks the solstice, reminding us that today is the shortest day of the year. And right on cue, *La Stampa* warns that the unseasonably warm "tongue" of air we mentioned yesterday is about to be cut off by a cold snap, with snow finally predicted for the Northwest just in time for Christmas.
