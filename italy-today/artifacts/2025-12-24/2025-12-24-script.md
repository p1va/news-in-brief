**SECTION 1: THE LEAD**

We begin with a country split between a white Christmas, a wet Christmas, and a tragic one. While the festive lights are on, the operational reality of the peninsula is currently defined by a severe "polar vortex."

*Il Sole 24 Ore* and *Corriere della Sera* report that the holiday exodus is facing a dual threat: gridlock and rising water. The A1 motorway—the country's spinal cord—saw eleven kilometers of queues today following a tanker explosion, while the rail network is suffering delays of over 90 minutes on the high-speed lines to Rome and Naples. But the real anxiety is in the north: The Civil Protection agency has issued a "Red Alert" for parts of Emilia-Romagna—specifically the Bologna area—where river levels are surging again. For a region still traumatized by last year’s floods, the holidays are beginning with sandbags rather than spumante.

Meanwhile, tragically, the Mediterranean does not take a holiday break. *La Repubblica* and the NGO Sea Watch report a massive shipwreck off the coast of Libya, with an estimated 116 migrants dead or missing and only a single survivor pulled from the water. The opposition press is framing this as a "Christmas Eve soaked in blood," using it to attack the government’s deterrence strategy right as families sit down for dinner. The Right-wing press, however, looks straight past the tragedy to the culprits. *La Verità* runs a scathing headline suggesting that, in the eyes of the Left, smugglers are "heroes," while *Il Giornale* focuses its coverage on the "pardoning" of a smuggler by President Mattarella yesterday. Two completely different realities, separated by a stretch of sea.

**SECTION 2: THE ROUNDUP**

Moving from the coast to the countryside, we turn to a story that has unexpectedly become a ferocious cultural battleground: the case of the "Family in the Woods."

As you may recall from social media, a Finnish-German couple living a primitive, "off-grid" lifestyle in the Italian woods had their children removed by the state. Today, the courts granted the father a two-hour supervised Christmas visit, but also ordered a psychiatric evaluation for the parents. The reaction is polarized. *Il Post* provides the cool-headed explainer, noting the children were found in hygienic conditions that alarmed social services. However, the hard Right has adopted this family as martyrs of state overreach. *La Verità* and *Libero* are furious, calling the psychiatric order a "poisoned Christmas gift" from the judges and quoting League leader Matteo Salvini, who calls the separation of the family "shameful." It is a classic Italian standoff: the cold bureaucracy of the law versus the sacred, untouchable nature of the traditional family unit.

Turning to the symbolic, Premier Giorgia Meloni has opened another front in the "identity wars," this time involving the Nativity scene.

Following yesterday’s controversy over the National Anthem, Meloni released a video message explicitly defending the *Presepe* as a symbol of "national roots" rather than just religious faith. *Libero Quotidiano* cheers this as the "Revolution of the Presepe," framing it as an act of defiance against a "woke" culture that wants to erase tradition—citing a Santa Claus deemed "too white" as their latest grievance. *Corriere della Sera*, playing the adult in the room, simply notes that for the Right, the Nativity has become a political flag to be planted. In Italy, even the Baby Jesus is subject to a parliamentary split.
